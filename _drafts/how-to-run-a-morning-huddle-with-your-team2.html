A morning huddle with your team can be the single most effective meeting that you have with your team.

The purpose of a morning team huddle is to check-in with your team about what your day or week will be looking like.

Your goal in the huddle is not to plan your week or projects -- that's what all the other meetings on your calendar are for.

Effective morning huddles are 'information sharing' meetings that delivers status checks from individuals to the rest of their team.

Here are a few ground rules to run successful morning huddles.

STANDING ONLY

As soon as people sit down around a table, they get into 'meeting mode'.

Meeting mode spurs debate and discussion which are great for planning, but are disruptive for when you just need to communicate status updates.

To run an effective huddle, ditch the meeting rooms and run a standing-only huddle right in your team's existing workspace.

ALL TEAM MEMBERS SPEAK

Ask each person on your team to share during the huddle.
Vocalizing individual status updates to your team creates greater commitment and task accountability.

If your company is too big (20+) to allow for individual updates in a 15-minute standup, then it's time to break off your huddles into departments or teams.

THE SHORTER THE BETTER

Under 15 minutes is great. Under 10 minutes and you're crushing it.
If huddles turn into 45 minute discussions, then you'll start to get a lot of glazed over looks from everyone who doesn't need to be part of the discussion.

Try scheduling your standups right before lunchtime (11:45am). Your team will have extra incentive to wrap things up quickly.

NO PROBLEM SOLVING

Why? Because problem solving requires debate, discussion, and review of all options. Schedule a separate meeting with others.
Prioritize information sharing instead of consensus building or debate.

If the meeting takes a turn toward discussion, quickly interject and suggest that folks debating a topic need to schedule a separate time to talk. Then ask for the next person's update.

WHAT TO SAY

Since the purpose of the huddle is to check-in with your team. What should you actually share during a huddle?

Here are some ideas:

  • What did you accomplish last week?
  • Top 3 priorities for the day/week.
  • Review your company or team's top 3 metrics.
  • Share roadblocks that you need help in (important: teammates should help AFTER your huddle, not during).
  • Recognize other team member accomplishments.
  • Share personal project wins.
  • Client updates.

Do not try and communicate all of these things during your huddle.

Start with just 2 or 3 of these items and ask your team to stick to them.

If you're initiating a morning huddle routine with your team, then lead the huddle by delivering a short, crisp status update to set the tone.

Building a morning huddle ritual will help make your team more effective by getting your team to vocalize what they're working on to their colleagues.

It also gives everyone a chance to know what's keeping everyone busy and should add depth beyond what your team task management tool is giving you.
